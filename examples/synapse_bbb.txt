We have data showing that the huma serum is toxic to neurons but not to other cells types such as
myocytes and hepatocytes. Thus, we hypothesize that the blood–brain barrier co-developed along the
complexification on neurons (we assume that neuronal specialization toward functional complexity is
what makes them fragile and vulnerable to blood components). We would therefore like to test the
idea of co-evolution (or co-adaptation) between neuronal activity genes (such as synaptic genes) and
genes involved in the formation of the BBB. There is some literature on links between synaptic function
and the brain-vascular/BBB, but I could not find direct studies of synaptic gene ↔ BBB gene co-
evolution.
We identified studies of The evolution of synaptic gene networks:
e.g., one study “Functionalization of a Protosynaptic Gene Expression Network – In the Light of
Evolution” documents how “protosynaptic” genes in early animals (even those without true synapses)
were co-opted and then regulated in networks as synapses evolved.
 Evolutionary constraint/co-expression of genes: For example, “Conservation and Coevolution in
the Scale-Free Human Gene Coexpression Network” finds that genes with many co-expressed
partners (hubs) evolve more slowly, and co‐expressed genes show similar rates of evolution.
 A direct synapse ⇄ BBB functional link: A recent study found that synaptic potentiation (in
cortex) leads to localized changes in BBB permeability, mediated via AMPA receptor signaling
and TGF-β, etc. (“Cortical plasticity is associated with blood–brain barrier modulation”.)
 BBB gene regulation / epigenetics: There is a review on epigenetic regulation of the BBB (tight
junction proteins, endothelial‐cell genes) showing how developmental and environment
influences shape BBB gene expression.
These pieces show: (a) synaptic gene systems are under evolutionary constraint and specialization;
(b) BBB genes are regulated and important for neurovascular/brain homeostasis; (c) there is
cross‐talk between neuronal/synaptic activity and BBB function. the paper showing that synaptic
activity modulates BBB permeability provides a mechanistic basis for why synaptic genes and BBB
genes might be under joint selective pressures. Hypothesis: as synaptic complexity expanded (e.g.,
in primates/humans), the vascular/BBB system had to adapt (greater nutrient delivery, tighter
protection, more active signalling) — this could drive coordinated evolution.

But I found no clear study explicitly mapping co-evolutionary signatures (e.g., correlated rates of
sequence evolution, co-duplication, or compensatory changes) between a set of synaptic genes and a
set of BBB genes across species. (The term “co-evolution” is used often in inter‐species interactions
(host-parasite, predator-prey) or inter‐protein complexes (e.g., mitochondrial/nuclear genes) but much
less so for two functionally linked gene sets within the same species (synaptic vs BBB)).

I found no clear study explicitly testing how selective pressures on synapse complexity/efficiency might
drive adaptations in BBB genes (to support more metabolic throughput, greater protection, etc.), or
conversely how vascular/BBB changes might permit synaptic expansions.
